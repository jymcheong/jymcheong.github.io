    Tactics Techniques & Procedures

    What is Tactics Techniques & Procedures?

    Before accomplishing a broad objective like "make more money", we often need to break it down to smaller parts or sub-objectives. It's the same for online criminals who want to steal or extort from victims.

    • We can think of Tactics as the sub-objectives, the WHATs (needs to be done) that lead to the main objective of let's say extortion through ransomware.
    • Techniques would be the skills and the HOWs (doing) to achieve those sub-objectives. With a ransomware campaign, it won't make sense just to lock a single workstation, it takes skills to install ransomware across the whole network.
    • Procedures would be knowing WHEN to do what. So let's say some ransomware gang gets into a vulnerable VPN server, then what's next? Is denying access to workstations to get victims' attention enough, maybe they need to find where are the backup servers, important files, extract them out of the network for a "double-extortion" & so on!

    Why is TTP important?

    Sun Tze: "If you know the enemy and know yourself, you need not fear the result of a hundred battles. If you know yourself but not the enemy, for every victory gained you will also suffer a defeat. If you know neither the enemy nor yourself, you will succumb in every battle"

    Without awareness of adversarial TTP:

    • Impossible to defend proactively & reacting to incidents (some degree of self-awareness, implemented some controls but don't know about enemies).
    • Don't even know attacks are happening (don't know about self AND enemies).

    Relationship with Attack Life Cycle

    Attack Life Cycle mental model provides a big picture view, & how different phases of attacks are related to the three necessary & sufficient conditions. Attacks are successful when attackers can use accessible methods (intersection between Threat Capabilities & Target Susceptibility) to reach the intended targets:

    With so many methods under the sun, how can we generalise into a mental model that is useful? We need to somehow group methods into tactics to know what exactly are the methods for?

    Tactics within stage 1 to 3 are the sub-objectives for the final objectives of Damaging, Stealing, Tampering & Denial.

    • Stage 1 & 2 are meant for remote Threat Actors since Insiders typically have direct access to resources
    • Procedures in TTP refers to the sequence of techniques to chained together
    • When attackers are undetected, they spread deeper (like cancers) into compromised networks & ultimately complete offensive objectives.
    • Stage 1 directly to 4 happens with low hanging fruits like poor configurations, exposed service network ports with default, stolen or weak passwords, attackers get lucky & completes objectives without much effort.

    Code Execution
    (Get in)    		 Using Metasploit or equivalent, attackers generate payloads that run arbitrary malicious codes on targets. They often test payloads against well-known signature-based products before the campaign, ensure they will be undetected.

    For the case of service-side vulnerabilities like FortiGate, the service port receives payloads & reacts by running malicious to typically call back to attackers' infrastructure.

    For client-side like a Windows network, malware which is short form of malicious software, is the most prevalent method of running malicious code.

    Abusing system tools (aka Living-Off-the-Land or LOL) to evade Anti-Virus & other host detection products is also becoming a norm as more companies adopt detection controls like Endpoint Detection & Response of EDR.    		 Lack of vulnerability management & preventive or corrective actions like restricting access (e.g. with Firewall) or patching.


    Even if Windows had no vulnerabilities related to software bugs, there are plenty of legit features that can be abused to achieve the next tactic of malicious Code Execution.
    Remote Command & Control (C2)
    (Get in)    		 Most of these malware & whatever offensive file-less techniques will report back to attackers infrastructure that further install backdoors into the target system.

    For the case of service-side & Linux servers, WebShells are common & for Windows clients, malware or file-less techniques all tend to report back to C2 over encrypted & common protocol like HTTPS (either SSL/TLS) because it is widely used (blending in) & unlikely blocked by firewalls.    		 Firewalls may be useful for incoming blocks but it can challenging to define granular egress (outbound) rules especially for larger networks.

    Once malicious code runs & reports back, it provides a control channel for adversaries to issue commands to run on compromised assets, which often act as pivots into the internal zones.
    Persistent Backdoor
    (Get in)    		 Attackers need a backdoor just in case the machine or device reboots or shutdown. The term "Persistent" means this backdoor will report back to C2 infrastructure again, to resume control sessions for attackers.

    Malware is commonly used in Windows, Linux, MacOS, Android & even Apple iOS. The only difference is that the difficulty & cost with the last being most expensive!

    There are also "file-less" backdoors that basically abuse system features & functionalities to achieve such "Persistence".    		 Many businesses DON'T have the budget for security monitoring service.

    It is almost certain that criminals will test their payloads against popular security products before a campaign. This means most backdoors are installed without being detected.

    At this point, attackers is ready to move on to the next stage of Attack Life Cycle: Spreading Laterally!
    Internal Recon
    (Stay in)    		 Unlike external reconnaissance, attackers have the option to survey the internal network zones & within the compromised asset.

    It's like getting a beach head into a virtual territory. Attackers have options to survey the compromised hosts or just start scanning networks to figure out the next steps.    		 There are lots of commands within various Operating Systems (OS) to gather information.

    Though such commands are not exactly vulnerabilities, it is hard to distinguish between user or attacker even if audit events are sent to a monitoring service.
    Credential Capture or Abuse
    (Stay in)    		 There are no shortage of tools to extract OS wifi passwords, browser session cookies, credentials & even email passwords.

    For example in Windows, it's also trivial to fake a logon or email retype password prompt & most users will happily comply.    		 For user touch points, there are also numerous ways to extract credentials like usernames & passwords from such endpoints.
    Privilege-Escalation
    (Stay in)    		 For networks that allow users to run as Local Admins (Windows) or Sudoers (Linux), there are also many free tools & techniques to escalate privilege. Doing so allows attackers to install higher privilege backdoors & extract more secrets or sensitive information. Even for networks that only runs Standard Users for client zones, unpatched & vulnerable endpoints are common such that attackers can escalate privileges.
    Lateral Movement or Internal C2
    (Stay in)    		 Lateral Movement in simple terms is about attackers moving from one compromised asset to another.

    In a Windows network, attackers almost certainly aim for Active Directory services & capture Domain Admin privilege. And once captured, attackers have full internal Command & Control over the whole network.

    For other networks like Internet-of-Things or even cloud services, attackers aim to infiltrate into management platform. For networks based on hub-&-spoke model, getting to the central hub gives further access to the rest of the network.    		 This is a sample report that covered infiltration to Domain compromised.

    Visualising Procedures

    In a complex network, attackers often have to decide what's their next move & this pattern tend to repeat as a series of attacks:

    Attacker -method1-> Target1 -method2-> Target2... TargetN

    When we visualise the tactics as a flow, it looks like this:

    • At Stage 2, the whole point is gain initial C2 & install backdoor so that sessions can resume after device reboots, because they need to Stay in Stage 3!
    • Once those two sub-objectives are met, attackers can start internal survey, typically with the immediate compromised asset like checking what kind of accounts are available, any credentials or session tokens to extract & so on.
    • Privilege Escalation is useful (e.g. extract more sensitive data from device) but not always possible especially when there isn't any escalation related vulnerabilities.
    • Attackers also often look at network routing information within the initial host or device, before scanning the network for other useful assets.
    • Moving laterally can be achieved by sending malicious payloads to more assets, causing Code-Execution or when privilege account captured, one can simply issue native commands to control target assets.

    *Code-Execution is essential for all other tactical aspects, even the initial asset to the next, other than abusing captured credentials, Code-Execution is usually the result of internal Payload Delivery, similar to what was described in detail in the earlier Hack-in-the-Box sample report.
